# How to use the Open Ring

The Open Ring is a diagnostic for structures where the wrong things are fixed. It names the capture pattern and finds the minimum action that would let the correct structure emerge.

You run it on markets, products, institutions, practices — anywhere you've spent enough time to see what's off about the arrangement.

---

## Three ways to run it

Same diagnostic, different amounts of help.

**AI version** — 10 minutes. You describe the structure in a few sentences. The tool scans five layers (Individual, Relationship, Institution & Market, Environment & Infrastructure, Culture & Narrative), finds every inversion, and produces a ranked report. Start here if you're exploring.

**Mechanical version** — 45–60 minutes. No AI. You answer every diagnostic question from your own knowledge. The framework applies the verdict logic and formats the output. Use this when you already know what you're looking at and want the sharpest possible result.

**Pen and paper version** — 60–90 minutes. Print the document. Same questions, same logic — you apply the verdict yourself. Use this when you want to work away from a screen, do it with someone else in person, or keep a physical record.

All three versions share the same Field. Records go to the same evidence base regardless of which version produced them.

---

## The structure of the diagnostic

Three instruments, run in order:

**The Gradient** asks what's fixed, what varies, who pays the cost, and whether the arrangement is genuinely stable or only appears to be. It names the inversion and finds the seed crystal — the minimum intervention that would let the correct structure emerge.

**SignalChain** takes that inversion and asks where in the information chain the problem sustains itself. It names the specific break point and the minimum action at that exact node.

**Field** records what happened when you acted. This is what turns a diagnosis into tested evidence.

## Running the mechanical version

Five steps on screen: Input, Gradient, SignalChain, Act, Field.

### 1. Input

Name the structure precisely. One line. "Surf fins" not "the surf industry." "Bitcoin custody" not "cryptocurrency."

Then describe what you know from direct experience: how long you've been in it, what you see that most people don't talk about, where the official story diverges from what you've seen. One paragraph — no format required.

If nothing comes to mind here, you probably don't have enough direct experience to run this version yet. Use the AI version first and come back when you know what you're looking at.

### 2. Gradient

Three pre-checks, then the diagnostic, then the gradient analysis.

*Pre-check one — resistance.* Four yes/no questions about whether incumbents can control education, distribution, finance, and legitimacy. These are the four mechanisms that keep a captured arrangement captured.

*Pre-check two — is the fixed part genuinely held?* Two yes/no questions. Would cheating on the fixed part cost significantly more than the gain? Would any attempt to cheat be visible? If either is no, what looks fixed is actually drifting.

*Pre-check three — does this have layers?* Some structures have completely different states at different levels. Bitcoin's protocol versus Bitcoin custody through exchanges is the clearest example — same structure, two opposite verdicts. If this is true here, each layer needs its own run.

*The diagnostic* — four questions. What's locked in place. What varies. Who pays when things go wrong. Whether the arrangement was designed or just accumulated.

*Gradient analysis* — where the pressure has built, what's holding the current arrangement in place, and what the smallest intervention would be.

The tool computes the verdict from your answers: capture immune, approaching immune, inversion confirmed, or inversion not confirmed.

### 3. SignalChain

Four nodes, evaluated in order: Signal, Channel, Ground, Reach. Each has three states — intact, broken, or partial.

The moment you mark a node broken or partial, everything downstream becomes indeterminate automatically. Don't evaluate those nodes. If information can't flow at node 2, whether it's distributed at node 4 isn't answerable.

At the first broken or partial node — the break point — write what's failing and the minimum action that would unblock it at that specific node. Not a general strategy. One concrete action at the break point.

### 4. Act

The tool shows the seed crystal and the intervention on one screen. This is the only step the tool can't do for you.

If the seed crystal points to something you can't actually do, name that constraint and find the next smaller action. A smaller real action beats a larger imagined one.

You can download your diagnostic here as a markdown file. If you come back later, upload it to resume from where you left off.

### 5. Field

Record what happened. Even if you don't know the outcome yet, record what you did and select "too early to tell." The record still counts.

Records are a commons — visible to all Open Ring users. No name or identity is collected, only the structure, the action, the outcome, and your experience level.

If you need privacy, don't submit. Download your diagnostic instead. A record on your device is still a record.

## Running the pen and paper version

Print the document. You need 60–90 minutes and a pen.

The parts are the same: Input, Gradient, SignalChain, Act, Field. The only difference is that you apply the verdict logic yourself. The document shows you how — the rules are printed directly below the questions they apply to.

After you act, submit your Field record to the shared form (the link is at the end of the document) or keep it on paper. A record that exists only on paper is still a record.

If you're doing the pen and paper version with someone else in the room, one person writes and the other asks the questions aloud. This is often sharper than either of you would produce alone.

## The one prerequisite

The tool is only as good as the experience you bring.

The framework organises what you already know. It does not replace knowing. A person with years of direct contact with a structure runs the diagnostic and produces something true. A person who has read about the structure runs the same diagnostic and produces something that sounds similar but is hollow underneath.

If you find yourself leaving more than two or three fields blank, you don't yet have enough direct experience. Use the AI version first.

---

## Three things to watch for

**Mixed equilibria.** Some structures don't have one correct arrangement they want to move toward. The natural state is several arrangements coexisting. In these cases the inversion reading is too simple, and a correctly placed seed crystal will produce partial movement rather than a clean shift.

**Rotated capture.** A seed crystal can succeed at releasing the current capture and immediately produce a new one in the space it opened. The diagnostic names what needs to change. It does not predict what prevents the next inversion from forming. Watch for who benefits from the new arrangement.

**The wrong-layer escape hatch.** When a seed crystal fails, it's always possible to say the fixed element was named wrong. Sometimes that's true. But the same move can protect the framework from evidence that would falsify it. If you find yourself re-specifying the fixed element after a failed action, ask honestly whether you're correcting a mistake or protecting a conclusion.
